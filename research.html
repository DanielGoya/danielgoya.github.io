Marshallian and Jacobian externalities in creative industries: Evidence from Chile

Specialisation in creative sectors is associated with higher own-sector sales and firm counts (Marshallian effects), but no cross-sector spillovers (Jacobian effects) are found — evidence from Chile suggesting caution in policy prescriptions for creative industries.

› Full abstract

This paper analyses Marshallian and Jacobian localisation externalities in creative industries using a panel of Chilean municipalities. We exploit variation in creative industry specialisation across municipalities to identify the causal effect on sales and firm entry in the same sector (Marshallian) and in other sectors (Jacobian). While specialisation in creative industries raises own-sector outcomes, we find no evidence of cross-sector spillovers — calling for caution in policy prescriptions that justify creative industry support on the basis of broader economic spillovers.

2023 PLoS ONE, 18(3)

The fear-increasing and fear-decreasing effects of a pilot policy to reduce fear of crime

with J.M. Benavente

A Santiago pilot (police + officials handing leaflets near a mall) reduced overall fear and actual crime among workers, yet paradoxically increased fear among those directly contacted — identified via difference-in-differences.

› Full abstract

We study a pilot programme in Santiago, Chile, in which police officers and government officials distributed security leaflets near a shopping mall. Using a difference-in-differences strategy comparing workers near the mall with those further away, we find that the programme reduced overall fear of crime and actual victimisation among workers in the treated area. Paradoxically, among individuals who were directly approached and handed a leaflet, measured fear of crime increased. These opposing effects highlight the complexity of crime-prevention communication and the importance of targeting in public safety policy.

2022 Technological Forecasting & Social Change, 174

The primacy of demand and financial obstacles in hindering innovation

with Andrés Zahler and Matías Caamaño

Using Chilean innovation surveys and an IV strategy across six barrier types, demand and financial obstacles dominate as inhibitors of firm-level innovation outcomes, with heterogeneous effects across firm size and sector.

› Full abstract

Using six waves of Chile's National Innovation Survey and an instrumental variables strategy, we estimate the causal effect of six types of innovation obstacles on three innovation outcomes. Demand-side and financial obstacles consistently emerge as the strongest inhibitors of innovation probability and intensity, while knowledge, market, cooperation, and regulation obstacles have smaller and more heterogeneous effects. Results are robust across firm size and sector, with larger firms more sensitive to demand obstacles and smaller firms more constrained by finance.

2021 Environmental Innovation and Societal Transitions, 41

Mining—The dark side of the energy transition

with Anabel Marín

The energy transition dramatically increases mineral demand, creating economic opportunities for resource-rich countries while simultaneously amplifying social and environmental conflicts — a blind spot in transitions research.

› Full abstract

The global energy transition requires vast amounts of minerals, including copper, lithium, cobalt, and rare earths. This creates significant economic opportunities for resource-rich developing countries, yet it also intensifies social and environmental conflicts associated with mining expansion. We argue that transitions research has largely overlooked this "dark side," focusing on decarbonising energy systems while neglecting the social and environmental costs embedded in the supply chains that make those transitions possible. We develop a conceptual framework integrating development and transitions perspectives, and draw on evidence from Latin America to illustrate the tensions and trade-offs involved.

2021 Economic Modelling, 102

The network effect of Chinese competition on what domestic suppliers produce

Import competition transmits through supply chains: as demand for a product falls, domestic multi-product plants drop the varieties they supply as inputs — especially small, low-productivity plants with low-markup lines.

› Full abstract

We study how Chinese import competition affects the product scope of domestic multi-product plants, focusing on the supply chain channel. Using Chilean plant-product data matched to input-output tables, we show that a fall in demand for a final good reduces the variety of inputs that domestic plants produce to supply that sector. The effect is concentrated in small plants with low productivity and low markup products, consistent with firms dropping their least competitive product lines first. This network transmission of competition represents an additional margin of adjustment often overlooked in the trade and employment literature.

2020 International Review of Economics and Finance, 70

The Exchange Rate and Export Variety: A cross-country analysis with long panel estimators

Export variety increases with a weaker (more depreciated) exchange rate and falls with exchange rate volatility — effects that are stronger for technologically intensive goods, using heterogeneous long-panel estimators.

› Full abstract

We examine how the real exchange rate level and volatility affect export variety across a large panel of countries over three decades. Using heterogeneous long-panel estimators that allow for cross-sectional dependence and slope heterogeneity, we find that a more depreciated exchange rate is associated with greater export variety, while higher volatility reduces it. These effects are heterogeneous across product categories, being particularly pronounced for technologically intensive goods. The results suggest that exchange rate policy can shape the extensive margin of exports and, in turn, the long-run structure of comparative advantage.

2019 World Economy, 42

Distance from core competences and new export survival: Evidence from multi-product exporters

with Andrés Zahler

Exports further from a firm's existing competence basket survive significantly less — especially in the first year — suggesting export diversification through existing firms should be gradual.

› Full abstract

We study the survival of new export products using a panel of Chilean multi-product exporters. Exploiting within-firm variation in the distance of new export products from a firm's existing competence basket, we show that exports closer to a firm's core competences survive significantly longer. The effect is concentrated in the first year of export and is robust to controlling for firm, product, and destination fixed effects. The results suggest that export diversification is constrained by organisational capabilities, with implications for trade and industrial policy.
